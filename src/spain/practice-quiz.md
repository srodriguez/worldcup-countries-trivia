<summary><strong>1. Name the famous unfinished cathedral in Barcelona, designed by Antoni Gaudí.</strong></summary>

**Answer:** The Sagrada Família

**Tip:** Construction began in 1882 and is still ongoing — expected completion around 2026–2032. Gaudí was so devoted to the project that he moved into the on-site workshop in his final years. He died in 1926 after being hit by a tram and was buried in the Sagrada Família's crypt.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>2. What Spanish dance form, known for dramatic footwork and guitar, originated in Andalusia?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** Flamenco

**Tip:** Flamenco was developed by the Romani ("Gitano") communities of Andalusia and has roots in Moorish, Jewish, and Spanish folk traditions. UNESCO added it to its Intangible Cultural Heritage list in 2010. The three core elements are cante (singing), toque (guitar), and baile (dance).
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>3. Which Spanish tennis player won 22 Grand Slam titles and retired in 2024?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** Rafael Nadal

**Tip:** Nadal is especially dominant on clay, winning the French Open a record 14 times. He's part of the "Big Three" alongside Federer and Djokovic. His successor, Carlos Alcaraz, has already won multiple Grand Slams in his early twenties.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>4. Name the Spanish city that is the capital of Catalonia and hosted the 1992 Summer Olympics.</strong></summary>

**Answer:** Barcelona

**Tip:** The 1992 Barcelona Olympics transformed the city's waterfront and are considered one of the most successful Games of the modern era. Barcelona also has several UNESCO-listed Gaudí buildings — Park Güell, Casa Batlló, and Casa Milà alongside the Sagrada Família.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>5. Which Spanish monarchs funded Christopher Columbus's 1492 voyage to the Americas?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** King Ferdinand II of Aragon and Queen Isabella I of Castile

**Tip:** In the same year as Columbus's voyage (1492), they also completed the Reconquista by taking Granada (the last Muslim kingdom in Spain). That single year — the Americas voyage, the end of the Reconquista, the expulsion of Jews — reshaped world history.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>6. What is La Liga, and which club has won it the most times?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** La Liga is Spain's top professional football league. Real Madrid has won it the most (36 times), followed by FC Barcelona (27 times).

**Tip:** Real Madrid vs FC Barcelona ("El Clásico") is one of the most watched sporting events on Earth. Both clubs are among the most valuable sports teams in the world, with hundreds of millions of fans globally.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>7. How many times has Spain won the UEFA European Championship, and name one of those years?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** 4 times — 1964, 2008, 2012, or 2024 (any one)

**Tip:** No country has won the Euros more than Spain. Their 2008–2012 era, with Xavi, Iniesta, and Xabi Alonso playing "tiki-taka" football, is widely considered the greatest international team of the modern era. Spain also won the 2010 FIFA World Cup.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>8. Spain has five co-official languages — Spanish, Catalan, Valencian, Galician, and Basque. Why does Spain have so many languages when most countries have just one official language?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** Strong answers will note that Spain was not always one country — it was formed by the union of separate kingdoms (Castile, Aragon, Navarre, etc.), each with its own language and culture. Unlike France, which aggressively unified around one language after the Revolution, Spain's regions retained significant autonomy and cultural identity. Basque is especially notable — it's unrelated to any other known language, suggesting it predates Roman conquest.

**Tip:** Language diversity is evidence of political history. Multiple official languages = multiple formerly independent cultures that weren't fully absorbed into a single national identity. Compare with France (one official language, strong centralisation) vs Spain (five, strong regionalism) — this contrast is useful in exam analysis questions.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>9. The Alhambra palace in Granada was built by Muslim rulers and is now one of Spain's most visited monuments. What does its survival — and preservation — tell us about how Spain relates to its Islamic past?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** Strong answers will note the complexity: the Reconquista ended Muslim rule in 1492, yet the Alhambra was preserved rather than demolished. This suggests that even conquerors recognised its artistic value, or that practical reuse mattered more than ideological erasure. Today it's a source of national pride and a major tourist attraction — Spain has come to celebrate a past it once sought to erase. This is a complicated relationship: acknowledging Islamic heritage while also celebrating the Reconquista that ended it.

**Tip:** "What does X tell us about Y" is an analysis prompt. Don't just describe the Alhambra — use it as evidence to say something about how nations deal with uncomfortable or complex pasts. The tension between pride in Islamic art and pride in the Reconquista is the interesting part.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>10. Spain built the largest empire in history in the 16th century, then lost most of it by the 19th century. Compare Spain's experience with that of another country you've studied — what patterns do you notice about how empires rise and fall?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** There is no single correct answer. Strong responses might compare Spain and France (both had large empires, both declined as naval and industrial power shifted), or Spain and Rome (rapid expansion followed by overextension and external pressures). Common patterns worth identifying: empires often fall due to overextension, economic drain of maintaining distant territories, rising competitor powers, and internal instability. Spain's case specifically involved dependence on silver from the Americas (which caused inflation rather than sustainable wealth) and failure to industrialise as Britain did.

**Tip:** Comparison questions are among the most common in history exams. The key is to identify specific patterns, not just list facts about two countries. "Both X and Y experienced Y because of Z" is a strong comparative structure. Pick the comparison you know best and make a clear argument.
</details>
