<summary><strong>1. What is the capital of Brasil?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** Brasília

**Tip:** Many people guess Rio de Janeiro — but Brasília became the capital in 1960, purpose-built in the middle of the country to develop the interior and reduce the dominance of coastal cities. It was designed by Oscar Niemeyer and urban planner Lúcio Costa and is itself a UNESCO World Heritage Site for its modernist architecture.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>2. Brasil is the largest country in South America. What is the name of the world's largest rainforest, which covers much of Brasil?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** The Amazon Rainforest (Amazônia)

**Tip:** The Amazon covers about 5.5 million km² and contains around 10% of all species on Earth. The Amazon River is the world's largest river by water volume. Around 60% of the Amazon lies within Brasil's borders — making Brasil's environmental policies a matter of global significance.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>3. How many times has Brasil won the FIFA World Cup, and which country have they won it more times than any other?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** 5 times (1958, 1962, 1970, 1994, 2002) — no other country has won it more

**Tip:** Brasil is the only country to have qualified for every single FIFA World Cup (all 22 tournaments from 1930 to 2022). Their 1970 team, featuring Pelé, is widely considered the greatest international football team of all time. Brasil wears yellow because of their many World Cup victories.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>4. What is the name of the famous carnival city in Brasil, and when does its carnival take place?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** Rio de Janeiro; Carnival takes place in February or March (before Lent)

**Tip:** Rio Carnival is the world's largest carnival, attracting over 2 million people per day during peak celebrations. Samba schools — neighbourhood groups that spend the whole year preparing — compete in the Sambadrome. The parade is judged on drumming, costumes, float design, and samba dancing.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>5. Which legendary Brasilian footballer, widely considered the greatest of all time, won three FIFA World Cups?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** Pelé (Edson Arantes do Nascimento)

**Tip:** Pelé won the World Cup in 1958 (aged 17 — the youngest World Cup winner ever), 1962, and 1970. He scored over 1,000 career goals. He died in December 2022. Many consider him and Maradona the two greatest players in football history, though Messi's recent career has added to that debate.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>6. What is the official language of Brasil, and why is it different from the rest of South America?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** Portuguese — because Brasil was colonised by Portugal (Treaty of Tordesillas, 1494), while Spain colonised the rest of South America

**Tip:** The Treaty of Tordesillas divided the "New World" between Spain and Portugal along a line of longitude. The eastern portion — which turned out to include most of what is now Brasil — went to Portugal. Every other country on mainland South America speaks Spanish as its primary language.
</details>

<summary><strong>7. Brasil is home to the Amazon, the world's most biodiverse ecosystem. Yet large areas of the Amazon have been deforested. Why do you think this creates a conflict, and who is affected?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** Strong answers identify the tension: deforestation drives economic development (agriculture, cattle ranching, soy farming) which provides livelihoods for Brasilians — particularly in poorer regions. But it destroys irreplaceable biodiversity, displaces Indigenous communities, releases carbon (accelerating climate change), and affects rainfall patterns across South America. Those affected include local Indigenous peoples (most directly), Brasilians dependent on rainfall for agriculture, and the entire global population through climate change. This conflict is between short-term national economic interests and long-term global environmental needs.

**Tip:** "Creates a conflict" + "who is affected" = two tasks. Identify the conflict clearly (development vs. conservation), then map out who's affected and how — local, national, global. Examiners reward students who see multiple sides rather than just picking one.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>8. Brasil was colonised by Portugal from the early 1500s until independence in 1822. The country imported millions of enslaved Africans — more than any other country in history. How has this history shaped Brasil today?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** Strong answers will note: Brasil has the largest African-descended population outside Africa; Afro-Brasilian culture (capoeira, candomblé, samba, carnival) is central to Brasilian identity; racial inequality remains stark despite this cultural influence — Afro-Brasilians are significantly more likely to be poor and less likely to hold positions of power. The legacy of slavery created a complex society that celebrates Black culture while often excluding Black people from opportunity — a tension Brasil continues to grapple with through debates over affirmative action and racial recognition.

**Tip:** History-shapes-today questions want you to trace a line from historical event → present reality. The most powerful answers show complexity: Brasil both celebrates and excludes Afro-Brasilian culture simultaneously — that tension is more interesting than a simple "slavery was bad" answer.
</details>

<summary><strong>9. Brasil hosted the 2014 FIFA World Cup and the 2016 Rio Olympics. Major sporting events are often said to bring economic benefits, but Brasil's experience was controversial. What arguments exist on both sides?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** Arguments for: infrastructure investment, international tourism, global visibility, national pride. Arguments against: enormous cost overruns, stadiums in cities that had no major football club (became "white elephants"), forced evictions of favela residents to build venues, money spent that could have funded healthcare and education. In Brasil's case, the events coincided with an economic recession and political crisis — public protests (the "Vinegar Revolution") against the spending erupted in 2013. The evidence suggests benefits are often overstated and costs fall disproportionately on poorer communities.

**Tip:** Both-sides questions want a genuine evaluation, not just a list. State the strongest version of each argument, then make a judgment: based on Brasil's specific experience, which argument is better supported? That's the difference between a good answer and a great one.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>10. Brasil, Germany, France, Italy, Spain, and England all have very different histories, yet all are playing in the same World Cup. What does the existence of global competitions like the World Cup tell us about how the world has changed since 1900?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** There is no single correct answer. Strong responses might note: international sporting competition requires peace and diplomatic recognition between nations (which wasn't guaranteed in 1900); global organisations like FIFA depend on global governance structures that are a 20th-century development; the World Cup creates a shared global event watched by billions simultaneously — only possible with modern broadcasting. Globalisation, improved transport, and international institutions have made it possible for countries with completely different histories and cultures to participate in shared global events. At the same time, the World Cup reflects persistent inequalities — some countries can afford better infrastructure and coaching than others.

**Tip:** This is a synthesis question — it asks you to step back from individual countries and draw a conclusion about global change. Use your knowledge of multiple countries from this course to make a specific argument. The best answers connect the existence of global sport to broader ideas about globalisation, international cooperation, and shared culture.
</details>
