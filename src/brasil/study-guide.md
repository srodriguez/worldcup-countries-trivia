## 1. Geography & Nature

Brasil is the **largest country in South America** and the **fifth largest in the world** — larger than the entire continental United States (excluding Alaska). It borders every South American country except Chile and Ecuador.

The country is dominated by the **Amazon Basin** in the north and west — a vast river system and rainforest covering roughly 5.5 million km², containing about 10% of all species on Earth. The **Amazon River** is the world's largest river by water volume (though the Nile is slightly longer). Around 60% of the Amazon lies within Brasil.

Beyond the Amazon, Brasil has the **Cerrado** (tropical savanna in the interior, the world's most biodiverse savanna), the **Pantanal** (the world's largest tropical wetland), the **Atlantic Forest** along the coast (now heavily reduced), and the great **Iguaçu Falls** on the border with Argentina — one of the world's largest waterfall systems.

The **São Francisco** is Brasil's most important river for its own population — it runs entirely within Brasil and provides water and hydroelectric power for millions.

Most of Brasil's major cities — Rio de Janeiro, São Paulo, Salvador, Fortaleza — are on or near the coast. The interior was historically underdeveloped, which is why the capital was moved from Rio to the purpose-built **Brasília** in 1960.

## 2. History — The Big Turning Points

- **Before Europe**: Brasil was home to hundreds of Indigenous peoples and civilisations long before Europeans arrived — estimates suggest 2–6 million people lived there in 1500.
- **Portuguese arrival (1500)**: Pedro Álvares Cabral, a Portuguese explorer, arrived on the coast in 1500 and claimed the land for Portugal. The **Treaty of Tordesillas (1494)** had already divided the "New World" between Spain and Portugal — the line happened to include eastern South America, which is why Brasil alone in South America speaks Portuguese.
- **The colonial era and slavery**: Portugal established a plantation economy based first on brazilwood (the country's namesake), then sugar, then gold and coffee. It imported more enslaved Africans than any other country in history — roughly 4–5 million people over 300 years. Brasil was the last country in the Americas to abolish slavery, in **1888**.
- **Independence (1822)**: When Napoleon's forces occupied Portugal, the Portuguese royal family fled to Brasil. When the king returned to Portugal, his son Pedro stayed and declared Brasil independent in 1822, becoming **Emperor Pedro I** — a unique peaceful transition compared to the violent revolutions elsewhere in Latin America.
- **The Republic (1889 – present)**: A military coup ended the monarchy in 1889. Brasil's history since has included periods of democracy, dictatorship (Getúlio Vargas in the 1930s–50s, military rule 1964–1985), and a return to democracy with the 1988 constitution.
- **Brasília (1960)**: President Juscelino Kubitschek ordered a new capital to be built from scratch in the interior, designed by architects Oscar Niemeyer and Lúcio Costa. It remains one of the most ambitious urban planning projects of the 20th century and is a UNESCO World Heritage Site.
- **Lula and the PT (Workers' Party)**: Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva, a former factory worker and union leader, became president in 2003 and lifted millions out of poverty during his first two terms (2003–2010). He was later imprisoned (controversially) and returned to win the presidency again in 2022.

## 3. Government Today

Brasil is a **federal presidential republic** — meaning the president is both head of state and head of government (unlike France or the UK, where these roles are separate). The president is elected for a four-year term, renewable once.

Brasil has 26 states plus the Federal District (where Brasília is), each with significant autonomy. The national legislature (Congress) has two chambers: the Chamber of Deputies and the Federal Senate.

Brasil's 1988 constitution established a multi-party democracy after the military dictatorship ended in 1985. The country has a proportional representation system that produces many parties and frequent coalition governments.

## 4. Arts, Architecture & Culture

Brasil's culture is one of the world's most distinctive blends of Indigenous, Portuguese, and African influences:

- **Carnival**: Rio de Janeiro's Carnival is the world's largest, attracting over 2 million people per day. **Samba schools** — neighbourhood groups that spend the year preparing — compete in the Sambadrome with elaborate floats, costumes, and percussion. Salvador's Carnival (more Afro-Brasilian in style) and Recife's Frevo are also famous.
- **Samba and Bossa Nova**: Samba grew in Rio's hillside communities in the early 20th century, combining African rhythms with European instrumentation. **Bossa Nova** (the "new wave") emerged in the late 1950s — soft, jazz-influenced, internationally famous through songs like "The Girl from Ipanema."
- **Architecture**: Oscar Niemeyer designed Brasília's futuristic government buildings in the 1950s–60s — the curved, space-age National Congress, the Presidential Palace, and the Itamaraty Palace are unlike any other capital city in the world.
- **Literature**: Jorge Amado (colourful novels of Bahia), Paulo Coelho (*The Alchemist*), and Clarice Lispector are internationally known Brasilian writers.
- **Christ the Redeemer**: The famous statue of Jesus with outstretched arms stands 38 metres tall on Corcovado mountain overlooking Rio de Janeiro — one of the New Seven Wonders of the World (2007). Built 1922–1931.

## 5. Food, Language & Daily Life

**Food**: Brasilian food reflects the country's diverse heritage. **Feijoada** (black bean and pork stew, typically served with rice, collard greens, and orange) is considered the national dish — with African roots. **Churrasco** (grilled meat, especially beef) is essential — Brasil is one of the world's largest beef producers and consumers. **Pão de queijo** (cheese bread made with tapioca flour) is a beloved snack from Minas Gerais. **Açaí** (a dark purple berry from the Amazon) has gone global, but has been a staple food for Amazonian communities for centuries.

**Language**: Portuguese is the official language, but Brasilian Portuguese differs from European Portuguese in accent and vocabulary — similar to the difference between British and American English, but more pronounced. Brasil also has significant communities of German, Italian, and Japanese descendants who preserve aspects of their ancestral languages.

**Religion**: Brasil is the world's largest Catholic country by population, though Evangelical Christianity and Afro-Brasilian religions (Candomblé, Umbanda) are growing. Many Brasilians practice a mix of traditions.

## 6. Sports

Football is not just a sport in Brasil — it's woven into national identity. Brasil has won the **FIFA World Cup 5 times** (1958, 1962, 1970, 1994, 2002) — more than any other country. They are also the only team to have qualified for **every single World Cup** since the tournament began in 1930.

**Pelé** (Edson Arantes do Nascimento, 1940–2022) is considered by many the greatest footballer of all time. He won the World Cup aged 17 in 1958, then again in 1962 and 1970. He scored over 1,000 career goals. More recently, **Ronaldo** (the original — 2002 World Cup winner), **Ronaldinho**, and **Neymar** have carried the tradition forward.

Brasil's style of play — creative, technical, joyful — is known as **"jogo bonito"** (the beautiful game), a phrase popularised by Pelé himself.

**Volleyball** is Brasil's second sport — the national teams (men's and women's) are among the best in the world, with multiple Olympic and World Championship titles.

**Formula One**: Brasil has produced three World Drivers' Champions — **Emerson Fittipaldi** (1972, 1974), **Nelson Piquet** (1981, 1983, 1987), and **Ayrton Senna** (1988, 1990, 1991). Senna, who died in a crash at Imola in 1994, is mourned as a national hero.
