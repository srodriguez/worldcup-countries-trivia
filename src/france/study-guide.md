## 1. Geography & Nature

France is often called “L’Hexagone” because of its roughly six-sided shape. Mainland France borders eight countries — Belgium, Luxembourg, Germany, Switzerland, Italy, Monaco, Spain, and Andorra — more land neighbors than almost any other Western European country.

The terrain is hugely varied. The **Alps** run along the southeastern border with Italy and Switzerland, home to Mont Blanc (4,808 m), the highest peak in Western Europe. The **Pyrenees** form the border with Spain in the south-west. In between lies a mix of flat farmland in the north, rolling hills in the center, and a Mediterranean coastline in the south.

Three rivers are worth knowing: the **Seine** (flows through Paris to the English Channel), the **Loire** (the longest river in France, famous for its valley of castles), and the **Rhône** (flows from the Alps down to the Mediterranean, important for transport and wine regions).

Don’t forget **Corsica** — a large Mediterranean island that’s part of France — and France’s overseas territories scattered across the Caribbean, South America (French Guiana), the Indian Ocean (Réunion), and the Pacific (French Polynesia). These aren’t just “former colonies” — they’re official parts of the French Republic today.

## 2. History — The Big Turning Points

A simplified timeline that covers most of what comes up:

- **Ancient Gaul**: Before it was France, the region was called Gaul, home to Celtic tribes, later conquered by the Romans (think Asterix and Obelix — based on this period).
- **Joan of Arc (1412–1431)**: A teenage peasant girl who claimed visions told her to help drive English forces out of France during the Hundred Years’ War. She helped turn the war in France’s favor before being captured and executed.
- **The Sun King (Louis XIV, reigned 1643–1715)**: Built the Palace of Versailles and ruled as an absolute monarch — a symbol of royal power and excess that later fueled resentment.
- **The French Revolution (1789)**: Started with the storming of the Bastille on 14 July. Ended the absolute monarchy, introduced ideas of liberty and equal citizenship, and gave France its motto: *Liberté, égalité, fraternité*.
- **Napoleon Bonaparte (Emperor 1804–1814/15)**: Rose from the Revolution’s chaos to rule much of Europe, then fell after defeat at Waterloo (1815). His legal code still shapes laws in many countries.
- **World War II & D-Day (1944)**: France was occupied by Nazi Germany from 1940. On 6 June 1944, Allied forces landed in Normandy — the beginning of France’s liberation.
- **Charles de Gaulle & the Fifth Republic (1958–present)**: De Gaulle led wartime resistance from exile, then founded today’s system of government, with a strong presidency.

## 3. Government Today

France is a **republic** with a President as head of state, elected every five years (renewable once in a row). The President shares power with a Prime Minister and Parliament. The current system, called the **Fifth Republic**, dates from 1958.

France is one of the “core six” founding members of what became the **European Union**, signing the Treaty of Rome in 1957 alongside West Germany, Italy, Belgium, the Netherlands, and Luxembourg. It’s also a permanent member of the UN Security Council and a nuclear-armed state.

## 4. Arts, Architecture & Culture

Paris is packed with landmarks that double as history lessons:

- **The Eiffel Tower** (1889) — built for a World’s Fair marking 100 years since the Revolution, originally meant to be temporary.
- **The Louvre** — once a royal fortress, now the world’s most-visited museum, home to the Mona Lisa and the Venus de Milo.
- **Notre-Dame Cathedral** — a medieval Gothic masterpiece, badly damaged by fire in 2019 and reopened after restoration in December 2024.
- **Palace of Versailles** — Louis XIV’s enormous palace, famous for its Hall of Mirrors.

In painting, **Impressionism** (Monet, Renoir, Degas, Pissarro) began in France in the 1870s, breaking from realistic styles to capture light and color, often painted outdoors. Their work fills the Musée d’Orsay in Paris.

France also has a strong film culture — the **Cannes Film Festival**, held every May, is one of the most prestigious in the world, awarding the Palme d’Or.

## 5. Food, Language & Daily Life

French cuisine is taken seriously enough that UNESCO recognized the French meal itself as cultural heritage in 2010 — and the **baguette** got its own UNESCO listing in 2022. Worth knowing a few regional specialties: croissants and quiche (everyday classics), coq au vin and ratatouille (traditional mains), and cheeses like Brie, Camembert, Roquefort, and Comté — France makes well over 1,000 cheese varieties.

On language: French is the only official language, but regional languages survive — **Breton** (Brittany), **Basque** (south-west, near Spain), **Occitan** (the south), and **Corsican** (Corsica, close to Italian). French numbers also have a quirk worth knowing: 80 is “quatre-vingts” (literally “four-twenties”) and 90 is “quatre-vingt-dix” (“four-twenty-ten”).

A few practical details: France drives on the **right** (like most of mainland Europe, unlike the UK), uses the **Euro**, and — like the rest of the world except a handful of countries — uses the **metric system**, which was actually invented in France during the Revolution.

## 6. Sports

The **Tour de France** is the country’s biggest sporting export — a three-week, ~3,500 km cycling race through the Alps and Pyrenees, with the leader wearing the yellow jersey.

**Rugby** is especially popular in the south (Toulouse, Bordeaux, the Basque region), and France hosted the Rugby World Cup in 2023. **Football** is also huge — France has won the FIFA World Cup twice, in 1998 (as hosts) and 2018. Paris hosted the 2024 Summer Olympics, which included swimming events in the cleaned-up Seine.
