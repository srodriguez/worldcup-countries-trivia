# 🇫🇷 France — Practice Quiz

<details>
<summary><strong>1. What major event began in 1789 and ended the absolute monarchy in France?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** The French Revolution

**Tip:** It started with the storming of the Bastille prison on 14 July 1789 — now France's national holiday, Bastille Day. The Revolution's motto, "Liberté, égalité, fraternité," is still France's official motto today.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>2. Which French military leader crowned himself Emperor in 1804?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** Napoleon Bonaparte

**Tip:** Napoleon went from army officer to Emperor in just over a decade. His legal reforms (the Napoleonic Code) still influence laws in many countries. He was finally defeated at Waterloo in 1815 and exiled to the island of Saint Helena.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>3. Which teenage heroine led French troops during the Hundred Years' War in the 15th century?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** Joan of Arc

**Tip:** Around age 17, she helped lead France to victory at Orléans in 1429. She was later captured and burned at the stake in 1431, but remains one of France's most powerful national symbols — canonised as a saint in 1920.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>4. What is France's national anthem called?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** La Marseillaise

**Tip:** Written in 1792 as a marching song for revolutionary troops from Marseille heading to Paris, its dramatic opening makes it one of the most recognisable national anthems in the world.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>5. What major WWII event happened on the beaches of Normandy in June 1944?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** D-Day — the Allied invasion of Normandy

**Tip:** On 6 June 1944, Allied troops landed on five beaches codenamed Utah, Omaha, Gold, Juno, and Sword. It was the largest amphibious invasion in history and marked the beginning of France's liberation from Nazi occupation.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>6. Which French scientist discovered radioactivity and won Nobel Prizes in two different sciences?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** Marie Curie

**Tip:** Born in Poland, Curie became a French citizen and discovered the elements polonium and radium. She remains the only person ever to win Nobel Prizes in two different sciences — Physics (1903) and Chemistry (1911).
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>7. France was a founding member of the European Union. Name at least two other founding members.</strong></summary>

**Answer:** West Germany, Italy, Belgium, the Netherlands, Luxembourg (any two)

**Tip:** These six countries signed the Treaty of Rome in 1957, creating the European Economic Community — the forerunner of today's EU. Knowing all six founding members is a common exam question: France, West Germany, Italy, Belgium, Netherlands, Luxembourg.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>8. The Eiffel Tower was built in 1889 and was only meant to stand for 20 years. Explain why it was kept, and what this tells us about how the value of things can change over time.</strong></summary>

**Answer:** It was kept because it proved useful as a radio antenna tower. Today it is France's most iconic landmark and the world's most visited paid monument — a complete reversal from when many Parisians called it an eyesore.

**Tip:** Good answers connect the specific reason (radio antenna) to the bigger idea: something's value is not fixed. The Eiffel Tower went from "ugly and useless" to "useful" to "irreplaceable cultural symbol" within a single lifetime. Examiners reward students who draw this kind of broader lesson from a specific fact.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>9. France has kept its language and culture strongly unified despite having regional languages like Breton, Basque, and Occitan. Why do you think France chose to make French the only official language, rather than recognising regional languages officially?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** There is no single correct answer. Strong responses will mention: the Revolution's goal of creating one unified nation of equal citizens (not a collection of regions); the role of a shared language in building national identity; and the historical fear of regional differences threatening unity. Some students might also note this policy has been criticised for suppressing minority cultures.

**Tip:** This is an "explain why" question, not a "what" question. The examiner wants reasoning, not just facts. A strong answer uses evidence (the Revolution, the motto, the idea of citizenship) to support a point of view.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>10. Napoleon's legal code (the Napoleonic Code) was introduced in France in 1804 and later spread across much of Europe and the world. What does this tell you about the relationship between military conquest and cultural influence?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** Strong answers will note that military power alone doesn't explain Napoleon's lasting impact — his laws were adopted because they were clear, modern, and useful, even after his military defeat. Countries that Napoleon never conquered (like parts of Latin America) still adopted versions of his legal code. This shows that ideas and institutions can outlast the armies that first carried them.

**Tip:** "What does X tell you about Y" is an analysis question. The trick is not to describe Napoleon, but to use Napoleon as evidence for a broader point about how influence works. The best answers step back from the specific fact and make a general claim.
</details>
