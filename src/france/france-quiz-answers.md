# France Quiz — Answers & Study Tips (Sets 1–3)

A reference sheet for all 30 questions. Each tip gives a bit of extra background — useful for remembering the answer *and* for impressing whoever asks the follow-up question.

-----

## Set 1

**1. What is the second-largest city in France, located on the Mediterranean coast?**
Answer: Marseille
Tip: Marseille was founded by Greek sailors from Phocaea around 600 BCE, making it older than Paris itself. It’s still France’s biggest port today and a gateway to North Africa — its historic heart is the Vieux-Port (Old Port).

**2. What was France’s currency called before it switched to the Euro in 2002?**
Answer: The French Franc
Tip: The Franc had been used for over 200 years. When the Euro arrived, several countries lost their old currencies at the same time — Germany’s Deutsche Mark, Italy’s Lira, and Spain’s Peseta all disappeared too, which is why these often appear as “match the old currency” questions.

**3. Name one regional language, besides French, still spoken in parts of France today.**
Answer: Breton, Basque, Occitan, or Corsican (any one)
Tip: French is the only *official* language, but these regional languages survive in specific areas: Breton (related to Welsh) in Brittany, Basque (unlike any other language in Europe) near the Spanish border, Occitan across the south, and Corsican (close to Italian) on Corsica.

**4. Which famous iron tower, built in 1889 for the World’s Fair, is a symbol of Paris?**
Answer: The Eiffel Tower
Tip: Engineer Gustave Eiffel built it to mark 100 years since the French Revolution. Many Parisians hated it at first and it was only meant to stand for 20 years — it survived because it turned out to be useful for radio antennas.

**5. Name the river that flows through Paris.**
Answer: The Seine
Tip: The Seine runs about 777 km from Burgundy to the English Channel. Nearly every major Paris landmark — Notre-Dame, the Louvre, the Eiffel Tower — sits along its banks. It was even cleaned up for open-water swimming during the 2024 Paris Olympics.

**6. What major event began in 1789 and ended the absolute monarchy in France?**
Answer: The French Revolution
Tip: It started with the storming of the Bastille prison on 14 July 1789 — now France’s national holiday, Bastille Day. The Revolution’s motto, “Liberté, égalité, fraternité,” is still France’s official motto.

**7. Which French military leader crowned himself Emperor in 1804?**
Answer: Napoleon Bonaparte
Tip: Napoleon went from army officer to Emperor in just over a decade. His legal reforms (the Napoleonic Code) still influence laws in many countries. He was finally defeated at Waterloo in 1815 and exiled to the island of Saint Helena.

**8. France was a founding member of which organization for European cooperation?**
Answer: The European Union (originally the European Economic Community)
Tip: In 1957, France joined West Germany, Italy, Belgium, the Netherlands, and Luxembourg in signing the Treaty of Rome — the “core six” founders of what eventually became the EU.

**9. Which mountain range forms the natural border between France and Spain?**
Answer: The Pyrenees
Tip: The Pyrenees stretch about 491 km and are home to the tiny country of Andorra. They’re older and generally lower than the Alps — don’t mix the two up.

**10. Name a traditional French dish known worldwide.**
Answer: Any of: Coq au vin, Ratatouille, Croissant, Baguette, Crêpes, Quiche
Tip: French gastronomy as a whole was added to UNESCO’s “Intangible Cultural Heritage” list in 2010, and the baguette specifically got its own UNESCO listing in 2022.

## Set 2

**1. What is the tallest mountain in Western Europe, located in the French Alps near Italy?**
Answer: Mont Blanc
Tip: At 4,808 m, Mont Blanc sits on the France–Italy border. The town of Chamonix at its base hosted the very first Winter Olympics in 1924.

**2. What type of government does France currently have, and what is its head of state called?**
Answer: A republic (the Fifth Republic), led by a President
Tip: The Fifth Republic began in 1958 under Charles de Gaulle and gave the President strong powers. The President is elected for a 5-year term, renewable once — Emmanuel Macron is the current President (elected 2022, next election due in 2027).

**3. Which teenage heroine led French troops during the Hundred Years’ War in the 15th century?**
Answer: Joan of Arc
Tip: Around age 17, she helped lead France to victory at Orléans in 1429. She was later captured and burned at the stake in 1431, but remains one of France’s most powerful national symbols.

**4. Which palace outside Paris, built by Louis XIV, is famous for its Hall of Mirrors?**
Answer: Palace of Versailles
Tip: Louis XIV (the “Sun King”) turned a hunting lodge into a massive palace. The Hall of Mirrors, with its 357 mirrors, is also where the Treaty of Versailles was signed in 1919, ending World War I.

**5. Which world-famous Paris museum houses the Mona Lisa?**
Answer: The Louvre
Tip: Once a royal palace, the Louvre became a museum during the French Revolution. It’s the most-visited museum on Earth, holding roughly 35,000 artworks — and is famous for its glass pyramid entrance.

**6. Name the famous cycling race held across France every summer.**
Answer: The Tour de France
Tip: First run in 1903, it covers about 3,500 km over three weeks, crossing the Alps and Pyrenees. The overall leader wears the iconic yellow jersey (“maillot jaune”).

**7. What is France’s national anthem called?**
Answer: La Marseillaise
Tip: Written in 1792 as a marching song for revolutionary troops, its dramatic melody makes it one of the most recognizable anthems in the world.

**8. Which 19th-century art movement, including Claude Monet, focused on light and color?**
Answer: Impressionism
Tip: Impressionists painted outdoors to capture fleeting effects of light, often with quick brushstrokes. The movement’s name comes from Monet’s painting “Impression, Sunrise.” Many works are displayed at the Musée d’Orsay.

**9. On which side of the road do people drive in France?**
Answer: The right-hand side
Tip: Most of continental Europe (France, Germany, Italy, Spain) drives on the right — only the UK and Ireland drive on the left. Handy to know if your family ever rents a car in Europe.

**10. Name one French overseas territory located outside Europe.**
Answer: Examples — French Guiana, Guadeloupe, Martinique, Réunion, French Polynesia
Tip: These are full parts of the French Republic (not colonies), so people there are French citizens, vote in French elections, and use the Euro — even though they’re thousands of kilometers from Paris.

## Set 3

**1. What major WWII event happened on the beaches of Normandy in June 1944?**
Answer: D-Day (the Allied invasion of Normandy)
Tip: On 6 June 1944, Allied troops landed on five beaches codenamed Utah, Omaha, Gold, Juno, and Sword — the largest amphibious invasion in history, marking the start of France’s liberation.

**2. Who led the Free French Forces in WWII and later founded the Fifth Republic?**
Answer: Charles de Gaulle
Tip: De Gaulle broadcast resistance appeals from exile in London during the war, then became President in 1958 and created the current Fifth Republic — linking two major chapters of French history.

**3. Which is France’s longest river, known for the châteaux along its valley?**
Answer: The Loire
Tip: At roughly 1,012 km, the Loire is France’s longest river. Its valley contains over 300 castles, including the famous Château de Chambord, and is nicknamed the “Garden of France.”

**4. Which large Mediterranean island, the birthplace of Napoleon, belongs to France?**
Answer: Corsica
Tip: Corsica became French in 1768 — just a year before Napoleon was born in its capital, Ajaccio, in 1769. It still has its own language and a strong regional identity.

**5. Which French city hosts a world-famous annual film festival?**
Answer: Cannes
Tip: Held every May, the Cannes Film Festival is one of the most prestigious in the world. Its top prize is the “Palme d’Or,” awarded to films from many different countries.

**6. Which French scientist discovered radioactivity and won Nobel Prizes in two different sciences?**
Answer: Marie Curie
Tip: Born in Poland, Curie became a French citizen and discovered the elements polonium and radium. She remains the only person ever to win Nobel Prizes in two different sciences (Physics and Chemistry).

**7. What is the name of the Gothic cathedral in Paris that caught fire in 2019?**
Answer: Notre-Dame Cathedral
Tip: Begun in the 12th century, Notre-Dame’s roof and spire were destroyed in an April 2019 fire. After a major restoration effort, it reopened to the public in December 2024.

**8. Which sport with an oval ball is especially popular in southern France?**
Answer: Rugby
Tip: Rugby is a big deal around Toulouse, Bordeaux, and the Basque region. France hosted the Rugby World Cup in 2023, with matches across the country.

**9. Which system of measurement (meters, liters, kilograms) was introduced in France during the Revolution and is now used worldwide?**
Answer: The metric system
Tip: It was created to replace a messy patchwork of local units. The original meter was defined as one ten-millionth of the distance from the North Pole to the Equator. Almost every country uses it today — the US is one notable exception.

**10. Name a French cheese that’s famous worldwide.**
Answer: Examples — Brie, Camembert, Roquefort, Comté
Tip: France makes over 1,000 types of cheese. Many have “AOC” status, meaning they can legally only be made in a specific region using traditional methods — Roquefort, for example, must be aged in caves near the village of Roquefort.
