<summary><strong>1. What is the capital of England?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** London

**Tip:** London is one of the world's most visited cities and has been a major settlement for over 2,000 years вЂ” the Romans founded it as "Londinium" around 43 AD. Today it's home to roughly 9 million people and is a global centre for finance, culture, and tourism.

<summary><strong>2. What river flows through the centre of London?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** The Thames

**Tip:** The Thames runs about 346 km from the Cotswolds to the North Sea. Iconic landmarks along its banks include the Houses of Parliament, Big Ben, the Tower of London, and Tower Bridge. The river was so polluted in the 19th century it caused "The Great Stink" (1858) вЂ” which finally forced the construction of London's modern sewer system.
</details>

<summary><strong>3. In which year did England win the FIFA World Cup, and where was it held?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** 1966, held in England

**Tip:** England beat West Germany 4вЂ“2 in the final at Wembley Stadium. It remains England's only major international football trophy. The tournament is still a defining cultural event for older generations in England вЂ” and a source of frustration that it hasn't been repeated since.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>4. Which famous playwright, born in Stratford-upon-Avon in 1564, wrote Romeo and Juliet, Hamlet, and Macbeth?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** William Shakespeare

**Tip:** Shakespeare wrote 37 plays and 154 sonnets. His works have been translated into every major language and are performed more often than those of any other playwright. He also invented or popularised hundreds of words and phrases still used today вЂ” "bedroom," "eyeball," "break the ice," "heart of gold."
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>5. England is part of the United Kingdom. Name the four countries that make up the UK.</strong></summary>

**Answer:** England, Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland

**Tip:** England is the largest and most populous of the four, which is why "England" and "the UK" are often confused вЂ” but they are not the same thing. Each country has its own culture, flag, and (for Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland) its own devolved parliament or assembly.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>6. What document, signed in 1215, limited the power of the English king and is considered a foundation of modern democracy?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** The Magna Carta (Great Charter)

**Tip:** King John was forced by rebellious barons to sign the Magna Carta at Runnymede. It established that even the king was subject to the law вЂ” a revolutionary idea at the time. It directly influenced the US Constitution, the French Declaration of the Rights of Man, and the UN's Universal Declaration of Human Rights.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>7. Which English scientist described the laws of gravity and motion in the 17th century, reportedly inspired by watching an apple fall from a tree?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** Isaac Newton

**Tip:** Newton published his *Principia Mathematica* in 1687, describing the laws of motion and universal gravitation. He also independently developed calculus and made major advances in optics. Einstein later built on Newton's work вЂ” though Einstein's theory of general relativity revised some of Newton's assumptions for extreme conditions.
</details>

<summary><strong>8. The Magna Carta limited the king's power in 1215, yet England still has a monarchy today. Explain how the role of the English monarch has changed over the past 800 years.</strong></summary>

**Answer:** Strong answers will trace the shift from absolute monarchy (the king's word was law) through the Magna Carta (king subject to law), the English Civil War and execution of Charles I (1649), the Glorious Revolution (1688 вЂ” parliament permanently supreme), and the Victorian era onwards (monarch as ceremonial figurehead). Today the monarch reigns but does not govern вЂ” real power lies with the elected Parliament and Prime Minister.

**Tip:** This is an "explain how X changed" question вЂ” it wants a process, not just a before-and-after. The strongest answers identify turning points (Magna Carta, Civil War, Glorious Revolution) and explain why each one mattered, rather than just listing events.
</details>

<summary><strong>9. England was at the centre of the Industrial Revolution in the 18th and 19th centuries. Why do you think England industrialised before other countries, and what were the consequences?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** Strong answers might identify: coal and iron ore deposits close to each other and to water transport; a political system that protected property rights and rewarded innovation; empire providing raw materials and markets; a history of trade and banking providing capital. Consequences: rapid urbanisation, child labour, pollution and terrible living conditions for the poor вЂ” but also mass production, railways, steamships, and eventually higher living standards. England's early industrialisation gave it enormous global power in the 19th century.

**Tip:** "Why did X happen first" and "what were the consequences" are two separate questions bundled together. Answer both. Examiners reward students who can move from causes to effects, not just describe one or the other.
</details>

<summary><strong>10. England once controlled the largest empire in history, covering about a quarter of the world's land. Today, English is spoken by over 1.5 billion people worldwide. What does the spread of the English language tell us about the long-term impact of empire?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** Strong answers will note that language is one of the most durable legacies of empire вЂ” long after political control ends, language persists because it's embedded in education systems, governments, and trade. English became the global language of business, science, and the internet partly because of the British Empire and partly because of American economic and cultural power in the 20th century. The spread of English created connections but also displaced local languages вЂ” a complex legacy with both benefits (global communication) and costs (cultural loss).

**Tip:** This question asks you to use a specific fact (English as a global language) to say something about how empires leave legacies. The best answers acknowledge complexity вЂ” the spread of English isn't simply "good" or "bad," and the reasons for it involve both the British Empire and American influence. Showing that complexity is what earns top marks.
</details>
