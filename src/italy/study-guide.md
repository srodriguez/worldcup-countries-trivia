## 1. Geography & Nature

Italy is instantly recognisable on a map — a **boot-shaped peninsula** extending into the Mediterranean Sea, with the toe pointed at Sicily. It borders France, Switzerland, Austria, and Slovenia to the north.

Two major island groups belong to Italy: **Sicily** (the largest island in the Mediterranean) and **Sardinia**. The country also completely surrounds two tiny independent states: **Vatican City** (inside Rome) and **San Marino** (in the northeast).

Two mountain ranges define the landscape: the **Alps** form the northern border, and the **Apennines** run like a spine down the full length of the peninsula. Italy has active volcanoes: **Mount Vesuvius** (near Naples), **Mount Etna** (Sicily — the largest active volcano in Europe), and **Stromboli** (a volcanic island).

Major rivers include the **Po** (north — Italy's longest river, flows through the industrial Po Valley), the **Arno** (flows through Florence), and the **Tiber** (flows through Rome).

## 2. History — The Big Turning Points

- **Ancient Rome (509 BC – 476 AD):** Rome began as a city-state, became a republic, then the Roman Empire — at its height controlling much of Europe, North Africa, and the Middle East. Julius Caesar, Augustus, and Hadrian were among the most famous rulers. The Western Roman Empire collapsed in 476 AD.
- **The Renaissance (14th–17th centuries):** A flowering of art, science, and culture centred in Italian city-states, especially Florence (funded by the Medici banking family). Leonardo da Vinci, Michelangelo, Raphael, and Galileo all worked during this period.
- **Italian Unification — Risorgimento (1861):** Italy was unified under King Victor Emmanuel II. The military campaigns of Giuseppe Garibaldi and his "Redshirts" were decisive in bringing the south under one government.
- **Mussolini & World War II (1922–1945):** Benito Mussolini's fascist government allied with Hitler's Germany. Italy switched sides in 1943 after Allied invasion; Mussolini was captured and killed in 1945.
- **Republic (from 1946):** After WWII, Italians voted to abolish the monarchy and became a republic. Italy was a founding member of the European Economic Community in 1957.

## 3. Government Today

Italy is a **parliamentary republic**. The **President** is the head of state (largely ceremonial, elected by parliament). The **Prime Minister** is the head of government with real executive power. Italy's parliament is bicameral — the **Senate** and the **Chamber of Deputies**.

Italian politics has been notoriously unstable: since 1946 Italy has had over 65 governments. This is partly a result of a proportional electoral system that produces coalition governments. Giorgia Meloni, who took office in 2022, is Italy's first female Prime Minister.

## 4. Arts, Architecture & Culture

Italy has more UNESCO World Heritage Sites than any other country — a testament to its extraordinary artistic heritage.

- **Renaissance Art:** Michelangelo painted the Sistine Chapel ceiling (1508–1512) and sculpted *David*. Leonardo da Vinci painted the *Mona Lisa* and *The Last Supper*. Botticelli created *The Birth of Venus*, now in the Uffizi Gallery in Florence.
- **Architecture:** The **Colosseum** (Rome, 80 AD) held 50,000–80,000 spectators for gladiatorial combat. The **Pantheon** (Rome) has one of the world's best-preserved ancient domes. The **Leaning Tower of Pisa** is a freestanding bell tower with a famous tilt caused by soft ground. **St Peter's Basilica** in Vatican City, partly designed by Michelangelo, is the world's largest church.
- **Opera:** Opera was invented in Italy around 1600. Giuseppe Verdi (*Aida*, *Rigoletto*) and Giacomo Puccini (*La Bohème*, *Tosca*) are the most performed composers worldwide. **La Scala** in Milan is one of the world's most prestigious opera houses.
- **Fashion:** Milan is a global fashion capital alongside New York, London, and Paris. Major Italian brands include Armani, Versace, Gucci, Prada, Fendi, and Dolce & Gabbana.
- **Film:** The **Cannes Film Festival** (in France) always features Italian entries, but Italy has its own prestigious **Venice Film Festival** — the world's oldest, dating from 1932. Director Federico Fellini (*La Dolce Vita*) is among the greatest in film history.

## 5. Food, Language & Daily Life

Italian cuisine is one of the most loved in the world — and extremely regional. **Pizza** originated in Naples (Pizza Margherita was created in 1889 for Queen Margherita of Savoy — its red, white, and green colours match the Italian flag). **Pasta** comes in dozens of shapes, each traditionally paired with a specific sauce. **Gelato** is denser and more intensely flavoured than ice cream. **Espresso** is the cornerstone of Italian coffee culture — a cappuccino is only drunk before noon.

Regional specialities are fiercely defended: real Roman **Carbonara** has no cream (eggs and Pecorino Romano do the work). **Risotto** is a northern dish. **Tiramisu** (literally "pick me up") was invented in the Veneto region in the 1960s–70s. Over 1,000 types of cheese are made in Italy.

Italian is spoken by about 85 million people worldwide and evolved directly from Latin. Regional dialects — Venetian, Sicilian, Neapolitan — can be so different that speakers sometimes struggle to understand each other.

## 6. Sports

**Football:** Italy has won the **FIFA World Cup 4 times** (1934, 1938, 1982, 2006) and is known for its defensive style of play ("catenaccio"). Italy won **UEFA Euro 2020** (played in 2021). The national team is nicknamed the *Azzurri* (Blues).

**Cycling:** The **Giro d'Italia** is one of cycling's three Grand Tours. The overall leader wears a pink jersey (*maglia rosa*), covering approximately 3,500 km over three weeks.

**Formula 1:** **Ferrari**, based in Maranello, is the most successful team in F1 history. The Italian Grand Prix at Monza (the "Temple of Speed") is one of the oldest races on the calendar. The Monza crowd (the *tifosi*) are among the most passionate in motorsport.
