<details>
<summary><strong>1. Which ancient structure in Rome, completed in 80 AD, hosted gladiator fights?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** The Colosseum

**Tip:** The Colosseum held 50,000–80,000 spectators for gladiatorial combat, animal hunts, and even mock sea battles. It's the largest amphitheatre ever built and one of the world's most visited landmarks — over 7 million visitors a year.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>2. Which Italian city is known as the "City of Canals"?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** Venice

**Tip:** Venice is built on 118 small islands connected by around 400 bridges. There are no cars — boats, gondolas, and walking are the only ways to get around. The city is slowly sinking and is regularly flooded by "acqua alta" (high water).
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>3. Which Italian artist, scientist, and inventor painted the Mona Lisa?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** Leonardo da Vinci

**Tip:** Leonardo was the ultimate "Renaissance man" — painter, sculptor, architect, engineer, and scientist. He filled notebooks with inventions centuries ahead of their time. The Mona Lisa hangs in the Louvre in Paris and is the world's most visited artwork.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>4. What independent city-state within Rome is the headquarters of the Catholic Church?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** Vatican City

**Tip:** At about 0.44 km², Vatican City is the smallest country in the world. It has its own government (the Pope), currency, and postal service. It became an independent state in 1929 under a treaty with Mussolini's government.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>5. What empire, centred in Rome, once controlled much of Europe, North Africa, and the Middle East?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** The Roman Empire

**Tip:** At its largest under Emperor Trajan (~117 AD), the Roman Empire covered about 5 million km². Its language, Latin, is the ancestor of French, Spanish, Portuguese, Romanian, and Italian — making Roman influence still felt in the languages of over a billion people today.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>6. What famous Italian car brand is known for its prancing horse logo?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** Ferrari

**Tip:** Ferrari was founded by Enzo Ferrari in 1939 in Maranello, near Modena. It is the most successful constructor in Formula 1 history. The prancing horse originally belonged to WWI fighter ace Francesco Baracca.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>7. How many times has Italy won the FIFA World Cup, and name one of those years.</strong></summary>

**Answer:** 4 times — 1934, 1938, 1982, or 2006 (any one)

**Tip:** Italy and Germany are tied on 4 World Cup wins each. Italy's 2006 win is famous for Zinedine Zidane's headbutt on Marco Materazzi in the final — Italy won on penalties. Italy also won UEFA Euro 2020 (played in 2021).
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>8. Italy has more UNESCO World Heritage Sites than any other country. Using what you know about Italian history and culture, explain why this might be the case.</strong></summary>

**Answer:** Strong answers will connect Italy's heritage to multiple eras: Ancient Rome (Colosseum, Pompeii, Pantheon), the Renaissance (Florence, Venice, Siena), early Christianity (Vatican, Ravenna), and regional cultures (Baroque Sicily, Alpine landscapes). Italy has been a centre of civilisation for over 2,500 years, with multiple powerful cultures layering achievements on top of each other in a relatively small country.

**Tip:** The question isn't asking you to list UNESCO sites — it's asking you to *explain* using historical reasoning. A strong answer identifies the key periods (Roman, Medieval, Renaissance, Baroque) and connects them to the density of surviving monuments.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>9. The Roman Empire collapsed in 476 AD, but Latin — Rome's language — is considered "dead." Yet five modern languages descend directly from it. What does this tell us about how empires can leave lasting influence even after they fall?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** Strong answers will note that Rome's most durable legacy wasn't its armies but its language, law, and culture — things that outlasted the military collapse by centuries. French, Spanish, Portuguese, Italian, and Romanian all evolved from Latin because Roman rule lasted long enough to replace local languages. This shows that cultural influence can survive political collapse: the Roman Empire is gone, but Latin-descended languages are spoken by over a billion people today.

**Tip:** This is an analysis question asking you to draw a broad lesson from a specific fact. Don't just describe the Roman Empire — use it as evidence to say something about how empires leave legacies. The best answers distinguish between military power (temporary) and cultural influence (potentially permanent).
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>10. Pizza was invented in Naples, pasta has hundreds of regional variations, and Italy makes over 1,000 types of cheese — yet there is no single "Italian cuisine." What does this suggest about Italy's history as a nation?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** Strong answers will note that Italy only unified as a single country in 1861 — before that it was a collection of separate kingdoms and city-states for centuries. Each region developed its own distinct culture, dialect, and food traditions independently. Italian "national" cuisine is really a collection of regional cuisines that happen to share a language. This explains why a Neapolitan and a Venetian might argue that the other's cooking isn't "really" Italian.

**Tip:** Food can be used as evidence about history. Regional food diversity in Italy is a consequence of political fragmentation — the same pattern you see in its dialects and architecture. Connecting food to political history is exactly the kind of cross-topic analysis that earns top marks.
</details>
