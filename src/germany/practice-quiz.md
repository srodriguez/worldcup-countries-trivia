<details>
<summary><strong>1. What event on 9 November 1989 symbolised the end of the Cold War in Germany?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** The fall of the Berlin Wall

**Tip:** The Wall had divided Berlin since 1961, preventing East Germans from fleeing to the West. When East Germany suddenly announced citizens could cross freely, thousands flooded the checkpoints that same night. Germany was officially reunified on 3 October 1990 — now German Unity Day.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>2. How many times has Germany won the FIFA World Cup?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** 4 times (1954, 1974, 1990, 2014)

**Tip:** Germany's 2014 win in Brazil included a stunning 7–1 semi-final over Brazil — one of the biggest shocks in World Cup history. Their first win in 1954 is known in Germany as the "Miracle of Bern."
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>3. Who was Adolf Hitler and what period did he lead Germany?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** Leader (Führer) of Nazi Germany, 1933–1945; responsible for launching WWII and the Holocaust

**Tip:** Hitler rose to power through elections, then dismantled democracy. His regime carried out the Holocaust — the systematic murder of 6 million Jewish people and millions of others. Germany today has strict laws against Holocaust denial and takes this history very seriously.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>4. In which German city is the famous Gothic cathedral known as the Kölner Dom?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** Cologne (Köln)

**Tip:** Cologne Cathedral took over 600 years to build — construction started in 1248 and finished in 1880. It survived WWII bombing largely intact and is Germany's most visited landmark.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>5. Which German scientist developed the theory of relativity?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** Albert Einstein

**Tip:** Born in Ulm in 1879, Einstein published his special theory of relativity (E=mc²) in 1905. He fled Nazi Germany in 1933 and became an American citizen. His work underpins GPS technology, nuclear energy, and our understanding of the universe.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>6. Which Reformation leader nailed his 95 Theses to a church door in Wittenberg in 1517?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** Martin Luther

**Tip:** Luther's challenge to the Catholic Church sparked the Protestant Reformation, which split Christianity in Western Europe. His German translation of the Bible also helped standardise the German language across many dialects.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>7. What is the name of Germany's constitution, and what year was it adopted?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** The Basic Law (Grundgesetz), adopted in 1949

**Tip:** Designed to prevent another dictatorship, its "eternity clause" makes certain democratic principles impossible to remove — no future government can ever legally vote to end German democracy. Human dignity is explicitly protected as inviolable.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>8. Germany was divided into two separate countries after WWII. Explain what was different about life in East Germany compared to West Germany.</strong></summary>

**Answer:** West Germany was a democratic, capitalist country aligned with the USA and Western Europe. East Germany (the GDR) was a communist one-party state aligned with the Soviet Union — citizens had restricted freedoms, the secret police (Stasi) monitored millions of people, and living standards were lower.

**Tip:** A strong answer contrasts both political systems (democracy vs. communist dictatorship) and everyday life (freedom of movement, consumer goods, surveillance). The Berlin Wall was literally built to stop East Germans leaving — which tells you a lot about which side people wanted to be on.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>9. Germany's Basic Law was deliberately designed to prevent another dictatorship after Hitler. What specific features do you think make it different from ordinary laws?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** Strong answers will identify: the "eternity clause" (certain principles cannot be changed by any vote), the explicit protection of human dignity as the first article, the ban on parties that seek to destroy democracy, and the Constitutional Court's power to strike down laws. These features reflect lessons learned directly from how Hitler used legal means to dismantle the Weimar Republic.

**Tip:** This is a "what makes X different" question — it asks for analysis, not just description. The key is connecting each feature to a specific historical lesson: Hitler won elections, so elections alone aren't enough; he passed laws to remove rights, so certain rights must be made unchangeable.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>10. Germany was responsible for starting two World Wars and the Holocaust, yet today it is considered one of Europe's most stable democracies and a leader in human rights. What does Germany's transformation tell us about how countries can change?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** There is no single correct answer. Strong responses will note: Germany confronted its past directly (unlike some countries), built strong institutions, accepted responsibility through education and law, integrated into European cooperation (EU, NATO), and benefited from economic recovery. The transformation shows that change is possible when a society actively chooses it — but it took decades and required honest reckoning with history.

**Tip:** This is the hardest type of question — it asks you to draw a general lesson from a historical case. The best answers don't just describe Germany; they use Germany as evidence to say something about how national change works. Examiners reward students who can move between the specific and the general.
</details>
