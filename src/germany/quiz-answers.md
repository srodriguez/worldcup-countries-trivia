# Germany — Quiz & Answers

Three sets of 10 questions. Score **8/10 or better to earn the prize**. Click each question to reveal the answer and tip.

## Set 1

<details>
<summary><strong>1. What is the capital of Germany?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** Berlin

**Tip:** Berlin became the reunified capital in 1990 after four decades of division. The Berlin Wall separated communist East Berlin from democratic West Berlin from 1961 to 1989. When it fell, people celebrated by chipping pieces off with hammers — fragments became iconic souvenirs.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>2. What is Germany's highest mountain?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** The Zugspitze (2,962 m)

**Tip:** The Zugspitze sits on the Germany–Austria border in the Bavarian Alps. Don't confuse it with Mont Blanc — that's Western Europe's highest peak and sits on the France–Italy border.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>3. What event on 9 November 1989 symbolised the end of the Cold War in Germany?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** The fall of the Berlin Wall

**Tip:** The Wall had divided Berlin since 1961, preventing East Germans from fleeing to the West. When East Germany suddenly announced citizens could cross freely, thousands flooded the checkpoints that night and the Wall was rapidly demolished.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>4. What was Germany's currency before the Euro?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** The Deutsche Mark (DM)

**Tip:** Introduced in West Germany in 1948 as part of post-WWII economic recovery, the Deutsche Mark became a symbol of German strength ("the economic miracle"). It was replaced by the Euro in 2002, along with the French Franc, Italian Lira, and others.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>5. Name a famous German composer from the 18th or 19th century.</strong></summary>

**Answer:** Johann Sebastian Bach, Ludwig van Beethoven, Johannes Brahms, or Richard Wagner (any one)

**Tip:** Beethoven was born in Bonn and composed his Ninth Symphony — including the "Ode to Joy," now the EU's anthem — after going completely deaf. He never heard it performed.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>6. Germany borders how many countries? Name at least two.</strong></summary>

**Answer:** 9 countries: Denmark, Poland, Czech Republic, Austria, Switzerland, France, Luxembourg, Belgium, Netherlands

**Tip:** Only Russia and France border more countries in Europe. Germany's central position made it a crossroads of European history — a key reason so many European conflicts started or ended here.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>7. What is Oktoberfest, and where is it held?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** A world-famous beer and folk festival held in Munich, Bavaria — despite the name, it starts in late September

**Tip:** About 6 million visitors attend each year, making it the world's largest folk festival. The tradition dates from 1810, when it began as a royal wedding celebration. Traditional beer tents (Bierzelte), giant pretzels, and lederhosen are the iconic images.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>8. How many times has Germany won the FIFA World Cup?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** 4 times (1954, 1974, 1990, 2014)

**Tip:** Germany's 2014 win in Brazil included a stunning 7–1 semi-final victory over Brazil — one of the biggest shocks in World Cup history. Their first win in 1954, defeating Hungary, is known in Germany as the "Miracle of Bern."
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>9. Who was the first Chancellor to unite Germany in 1871?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** Otto von Bismarck

**Tip:** Known as the "Iron Chancellor," Bismarck united dozens of German states under Prussian leadership through clever diplomacy and targeted wars. His phrase "blood and iron" defined his approach to politics.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>10. What famous fairy tales were collected by German brothers in the 19th century?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** The Brothers Grimm (Jacob and Wilhelm Grimm)

**Tip:** Their collection includes Cinderella, Snow White, Hansel and Gretel, Rapunzel, and Rumpelstiltskin. These weren't invented by the Grimms — they collected folk tales passed down orally for generations and wrote them down.
</details>

## Set 2

<details>
<summary><strong>1. What major river runs through Cologne and forms part of Germany's western border?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** The Rhine

**Tip:** The Rhine runs about 865 km through Germany. The Rhine Valley — dotted with medieval castles and vineyards — is a UNESCO World Heritage site and one of Europe's most popular river cruise routes.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>2. What is the name of Germany's federal parliament?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** The Bundestag

**Tip:** Germany has two parliamentary chambers: the Bundestag (elected by citizens) and the Bundesrat (representing the 16 states). The Bundestag meets in the Reichstag building in Berlin, which has a famous glass dome visitors can walk around.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>3. Which German city hosted the 1972 Summer Olympics — remembered for a tragic terrorist attack?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** Munich

**Tip:** Palestinian militants kidnapped and killed 11 Israeli athletes and coaches at the 1972 Munich Olympics. Despite the tragedy, the Games continued — a decision still debated today. The city's Olympic Park remains one of Germany's major landmarks.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>4. Who was Adolf Hitler and what period did he lead Germany?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** Leader (Führer) of Nazi Germany, 1933–1945; responsible for launching WWII and the Holocaust

**Tip:** Hitler rose to power through elections, then dismantled democracy. His regime carried out the Holocaust — the systematic murder of 6 million Jewish people and millions of others. Germany today has strict laws against Holocaust denial and takes this history very seriously.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>5. What is the name of Germany's famous castle, often compared to Cinderella's castle?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** Neuschwanstein Castle

**Tip:** Built by the eccentric King Ludwig II of Bavaria in the 19th century as a romantic fantasy retreat, Neuschwanstein directly inspired the Sleeping Beauty Castle at Disneyland. Ludwig died mysteriously in 1886 before the castle was completed.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>6. What type of government does Germany have?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** A federal parliamentary republic (with 16 Länder/states)

**Tip:** Germany's federal structure — where states have real political power — was partly designed after WWII to prevent dangerous concentration of power. The "Basic Law" constitution explicitly protects democracy as unchangeable.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>7. In which German city is the famous Gothic cathedral (Kölner Dom) located?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** Cologne (Köln)

**Tip:** Cologne Cathedral took over 600 years to build — construction started in 1248 and finished in 1880. It survived WWII bombing largely intact and is Germany's most visited landmark, drawing 6 million visitors a year.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>8. Which German racing driver won the most Formula 1 World Championships?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** Michael Schumacher (7 championships: 1994, 1995, and 2000–2004)

**Tip:** Schumacher dominated F1 in the early 2000s with Ferrari. Lewis Hamilton later equalled his record of 7 championships. Schumacher suffered a severe skiing accident in 2013 and has been out of public life since.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>9. When was Germany officially reunified after the Cold War?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** 3 October 1990 — now celebrated as German Unity Day

**Tip:** Reunification was remarkably fast — the Wall fell in November 1989, and within less than a year the two German states had merged politically, economically, and socially. The process of equalising living standards between East and West is still ongoing.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>10. Name a famous German food or dish.</strong></summary>

**Answer:** Bratwurst, Schnitzel, Pretzels, Sauerkraut, Black Forest cake (any one)

**Tip:** The Black Forest cake (Schwarzwälder Kirschtorte) comes from Baden-Württemberg. Germany has over 1,300 breweries and makes an extraordinary variety of bread — over 300 officially recognised types. German bread culture has UNESCO Intangible Heritage status.
</details>

## Set 3

<details>
<summary><strong>1. What is Germany's largest city by population?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** Berlin (~3.7 million)

**Tip:** Berlin is nearly twice the size of Hamburg (Germany's second city). Despite being the capital, Berlin is famously described as "poor but sexy" — known for arts, nightlife, and a lower cost of living than most major European capitals.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>2. Which German scientist developed the theory of relativity?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** Albert Einstein

**Tip:** Einstein was born in Ulm, Germany, in 1879. He published his special theory of relativity (E=mc²) in 1905. He fled Nazi Germany in 1933 and settled in the USA. His work underpins GPS technology, nuclear energy, and our understanding of the universe.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>3. What was the communist state that existed in eastern Germany from 1949 to 1990?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** East Germany — officially the German Democratic Republic (GDR, or DDR in German)

**Tip:** Despite "Democratic" in its name, the GDR was a one-party communist state. Its secret police — the Stasi — was one of the most extensive surveillance organisations in history, with files on millions of its own citizens.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>4. What printing technology did Johannes Gutenberg invent around 1450?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** The movable-type printing press

**Tip:** Gutenberg's invention, developed in Mainz, made books far cheaper and faster to produce. It helped spread the Renaissance and the Protestant Reformation across Europe. The first major book printed was the Gutenberg Bible (~1455), copies of which sell for over $25 million today.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>5. Which German city is home to BMW's headquarters?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** Munich (BMW); also Stuttgart (Mercedes-Benz and Porsche)

**Tip:** Germany is synonymous with car engineering. BMW stands for Bayerische Motoren Werke (Bavarian Motor Works). Other German giants include Volkswagen (Wolfsburg), Audi (Ingolstadt), and Porsche (Stuttgart) — all competing at the top of global automotive design.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>6. What is Germany's national anthem called?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** "Das Lied der Deutschen" — only the third verse ("Einigkeit und Recht und Freiheit" — Unity, Justice, and Freedom) is used

**Tip:** The full song has three stanzas but only the third is used officially today — the first two were misused during the nationalist and Nazi eras. Germany deliberately chose the verse about unity, justice, and freedom to reflect its post-war values.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>7. Which Reformation leader nailed his 95 Theses to a church door in Wittenberg in 1517?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** Martin Luther

**Tip:** Luther's challenge to the Catholic Church sparked the Protestant Reformation, splitting Christianity in Western Europe and leading to decades of religious conflict. His German translation of the Bible also helped standardise the German language across many dialects.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>8. What is Germany's constitution called, and when was it adopted?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** The Basic Law (Grundgesetz), adopted in 1949

**Tip:** Designed to prevent another dictatorship, the Basic Law explicitly protects human dignity as inviolable. Its "eternity clause" makes certain democratic principles impossible to remove — no future government can ever legally vote to end German democracy.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>9. Name a major German technology or industrial company.</strong></summary>

**Answer:** Volkswagen, Mercedes-Benz, BMW, Siemens, SAP, Bosch, Bayer (any one)

**Tip:** Germany has the largest economy in Europe and the fourth-largest in the world. Its "Mittelstand" — thousands of mid-sized, often family-owned specialist manufacturers — is frequently cited as the backbone of its industrial strength and export success.
</details>

<details>
<summary><strong>10. What is the name of the document signed in 1919 that ended WWI and imposed harsh terms on Germany?</strong></summary>

**Answer:** The Treaty of Versailles

**Tip:** Signed at the Palace of Versailles in France, the treaty forced Germany to accept sole blame for WWI, pay enormous reparations, and give up territory and its military. Many historians argue these harsh terms created the economic and political conditions that helped Hitler rise to power.
</details>
